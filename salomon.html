Rationale

This project focuses on reimagining how the courier carries their load by addressing the ergonomic inefficiencies and functional limitations inherent in traditional carry systems. Drawing inspiration from the Unix philosophy (Behind the building of unix operating systems such as MacOS, BSD & Linux)—which promotes building tools that do one job only and one job well, the design introduces a modular system of tools. Suckless Software- (keeping things simple, minimal and useable) is a specialised application of the unix philosophy directed towards the development of tools. These tools can be chained together to build systems that are both transparent and functional, allowing for flexibility via composition. This system balances accessibility, stability, and capacity, offering a versatile solution for various disciplines beyond courier work when considering the systems modular nature.

The aim is to create a system that evolves with its users, improving workflow and expandability while addressing the ergonomic inefficiencies and functional limitations inherent in traditional carry systems. By embracing principles drawn from both the Unix and Suckless philosophies, the system minimises complexity—eliminating unnecessary “bloat” and instead focuses on clear, intentional construction. In the world of design that balances function and aesthetics, overly complex patterns with extra parts that serve no purpose are a common sight. In IT terms, this would be called “bloat”. The suckless movement is born from a digital world using bloated software as the standard. from a design perspective, there is too little attention towards the philosophy of how something is constructed and this is an issue because bloated software always has more bugs just as a more complex garment construction has more loose ends and points of weakness. Suckless philosophy features inherent repairability.

While initially targeting couriers, the design is envisioned to resonate to a broader demographic, including professionals in construction, lifestyle hobbyists, and those operating in high-pressure environments (such as conflict zones or film sets) will benefit from the product’s modularity and transparency. Tinkerers will benefit from this system’s visible technology and repairable design. They are empowered to develop personalised modules and modifications according to their own requirements. This is a carry system rather than a “bag”. This is necessary because the market for tinkerers and makers is growing continuously, with each in search for a solution to their unique work style. The system’s modularity provides relevance across these varied use cases.

Rooted in a background in engineering, this design thinking moves beyond the conventional carry methods, presenting a configurable system of modular components. A stable brace and easily accessible containers anchor the design, enhancing both comfort and practicality for users. The visible exoskeleton structure ensures transparency in function, repairability, and user engagement. Complementing this approach is Salomon’s philosophy of visible technology, which celebrates the beauty of an exposed, functional framework. By openly displaying its exoskeletal structure, the design not only reinforces technical integrity but also invites users to engage directly with the system’s inner workings, encouraging dynamic use from a deeper understanding and connection between the product and user.

The concept draws inspiration from wearable technology, reimagining the bag as an extension of the body, through design philosophies of the software world. The design encourages a seamless integration into professional and personal workflows. In the same way that digital systems thrive on minimalism and transparency—avoiding unnecessary complexity that can lead to bugs—this product rejects overly complex construction in favour of clarity and efficiency. There is a distinct style in how a machine works, and by exposing and also highlighting the mechanics of its assembly using colour and material choice, the product communicates both functionality and aesthetic elegance, allowing users to immediately understand and adapt the system. Aesthetic detailing such as colour emphasises the integration of visible technology using overall neutral tone. Within this palette, the accent evoke the look of circuitry and hardware, reinforcing the industrial character which the form also reflects. Architectural precision moulded the silhouette and the system itself acts as a compatibility layer between human-centric ergonomics and the machined rigidity of utilitarian modules. Lightweight yet robust, the structure is designed to withstand diverse demands without compromising comfort.

Key details include modular components, intuitive assembly, and strategically placed reinforcement points (including a stabilisation system). Each element is designed to enhance usability and encourage customization. Complimenting this approach is Salomons philosophy of visible technology, which celebrates the beauty of exposed exoskeletal structure and metaphorically looking into the machine… inviting users to engage directly with the products workings. Visible architecture reinforces technical integrity and also encourages them to understand and work alongside the prescribed functions. There’s style in how a machine works rather than just the visual image of what it looks like.

Drawing from experience and knowledge from using decentralised networks: the carry case/parcel module of the carry system has graphics that role-play a near world where couriers are no longer operating at the mercy of large logistics companies. The courier has been decentralised and use protocols maintained autonomously (DAO) to compute exchange markets, addresses, tracking and insurance using decentralised ledger, “naming and certificate authority” (handshake.org), bringing control and efficiency to individuals. The world building includes the fact that these individuals would supply their own workwear/gear. On the specific container/parcel used in this physical project, the reusable parcel has a registered owner and has an assigned to an addressee. It will make it’s way to it’s destination by couriers collecting and dropping it off at nodes. reducing unnecessary packaging and providing freedom and fulfilment via work to all parties involved. Payments are distributed via “proof of work”/contribution using blockchain.

The design is a tool. Not only to create things, but also ideas. “Commodified Imaginations” (Dunne & Raby, 2013)
